About the Program

Mission: Broaden participation in geosciences through inclusive, flexible research experiences

Why It Matters

  • Addresses barriers to traditional REU formats
  • Builds awareness of geoscience careers
  • Supports a broad range of students
  • Creates inclusive research communities

Flexible Format

2 weeks fieldwork (January) at CSUSB, 2 weeks lab work (June) at UC, plus weekly virtual meetings and workshops throughout the academic year.

Study Areas

Research sites include the San Bernardino Mountains, San Gabriel Foothills, and San Timoteo Badlands - key areas for understanding San Andreas fault evolution.

Professional Development

Monthly workshops covering careers in geoscience, resume writing, ethics & data communication, and graduate school applications.

Mentorship

1-on-1 mentor meetings, peer support and cohort bonding, with both virtual and in-person engagement throughout the program.

Research Focus

Theme: Slip history and tectonic evolution of the southern San Andreas fault system

Sedimentology & Tectonics Track

Field mapping, sedimentology, and geochronology to understand basin evolution. Analyze middle Miocene to Pleistocene sedimentary sequences using detrital zircon U-Pb dating.

Neotectonics/Geomorphology Track

Drone-based LiDAR surveys and geomorphological analysis. Map Quaternary surfaces and calculate slip rates from offset geomorphic features.

Advanced Methods

Detrital zircon U-Pb dating, geomorphological and soil analysis, field mapping and sedimentology, plus professional development workshops.

Key Research Questions

  • How do individual faults organize in space and time?
  • How do long-term slip rates compare to recent rates?
  • What drives strain and landscape evolution?

Target Study Areas & Formations

San Bernardino Mountains: Active faulting and recent deformation along the main San Andreas system
San Gabriel Foothills: Quaternary surfaces and geomorphic markers of fault activity
San Timoteo Badlands: Upper Miocene to Pleistocene succession recording fault evolution
Key Formations: Cajon Pass, Punchbowl, and Quaternary fanglomerates providing slip rate constraints

CSUSB Faculty Mentors

Dr. Tandis Bidgoli - PI

Structural Geology & Thermochronology

Leads the REU program with a focus on fault mechanics, tectonic evolution, and inclusive mentorship. Her research spans active deformation in southern California and the Great Basin.

Dr. Claire Todd

Glacial Geomorphology

Studies glacial landscapes and climate change, with fieldwork in Antarctica and the Pacific Northwest. She mentors students in climate-focused geomorphic research.

Dr. Maria Henriquez

Structural Geology & Thermochronology

Investigates deformation timing and mechanics using geochronology and thermochronology. Guides students in interpreting tectonic histories through field and lab work.

Dr. Kerry Cato

Engineering Geology

Specializes in landslides, debris flows, and slope stability. Mentors students in applied geologic hazard analysis and field-based investigations. FAA certified drone pilot.

Who Should Apply

  • Junior-level geoscience majors
  • Community college students in introductory geology
  • Students with financial, familial, or time constraints
  • U.S. citizens or permanent residents
  • Strong interest in geoscience research

Application Materials

  • Online application with demographic info
  • Short essays on goals and challenges
  • Unofficial transcripts
  • Faculty references (for short-listed candidates)
  • Virtual interview (if selected)

Selection Criteria

  • Commitment to geoscience research
  • Barriers to traditional REU participation
  • Contribution to program diversity
  • Academic preparation and motivation
  • Clear research interests

Program Benefits

  • Professional development workshops

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need prior research experience?

No! This program is designed for students at all levels, including those with no prior research experience.

What if I can't attend both intensive sessions?

Both the January field work and June lab work are required components. We provide travel funding and accommodation to minimize barriers.

Is this program only for geology majors?

While we welcome geoscience majors, we also encourage students from related fields (environmental science, engineering, etc.) with strong interest in geological research.

How much time commitment is required during the academic year?

Expect 5-10 hours per week including weekly virtual meetings, individual research time, and monthly workshops.
